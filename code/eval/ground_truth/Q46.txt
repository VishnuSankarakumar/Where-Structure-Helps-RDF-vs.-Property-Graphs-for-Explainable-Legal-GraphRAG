There is a genuine tension. Recital (73) requires that human overseers can intervene, stop systems, and make informed decisions, while Recital (72) requires systems be designed so deployers can 'understand how the AI system works.' Yet Recital (12) acknowledges AI systems operate with varying levels of autonomy and adaptiveness, and Recital (75) recognises limitations within systems and unexpected situations. The Act attempts resolution through: instructions of use with illustrative examples (Recital 72), in-built operational constraints that cannot be overridden by the system (Recital 73), automatic recording of events via logs (Recital 71), and AI literacy obligations (Recital 20). However, the Act is silent on how to verify that human oversight is genuinely meaningful versus pro forma checkbox compliance. It does not define minimum competency standards, does not address automation bias (where humans routinely defer to AI), and for biometric identification merely requires two-person verification which could still be routine rubber-stamping. The fundamental question of whether humans can meaningfully oversee systems whose reasoning they cannot fully understand remains unresolved.
