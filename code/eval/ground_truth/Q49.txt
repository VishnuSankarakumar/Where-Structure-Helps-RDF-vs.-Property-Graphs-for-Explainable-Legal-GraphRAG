Consider a recommendation algorithm classified as a general-purpose AI system on a very large online platform. Under the DSA, the platform conducts systemic risk assessments and takes mitigating measures. The AI Act presumption of fulfilment applies. However, the platform does not provide the specific AI Act transparency information (instructions of use, capabilities and limitations per Recital 72) because it relies on the DSA presumption. If the algorithm then produces discriminatory outcomes in a high-risk use case (e.g., employment advertising), the presumption breaks down because 'significant systemic risks not covered by' the DSA have emerged. The Act does not specify: who determines when the presumption is rebutted, what the timeline is for compliance once systemic risks emerge, or whether liability accrues retroactively to the point the risk first existed. Recital (136) clarifies that AI Act labelling requirements do not affect DSA obligations on illegal content processing, but this creates a scenario where the platform is compliant with both Acts individually yet the combined effect leaves a governance gap. The AI Office has GPAI model oversight (Recital 162) and national authorities have AI system oversight, but the DSA creates a parallel supervisory structure with its own digital services coordinators, potentially leading to conflicting assessments of what constitutes 'significant systemic risk.'
