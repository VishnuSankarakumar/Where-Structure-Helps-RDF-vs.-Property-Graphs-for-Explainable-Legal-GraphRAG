This represents a genuine regulatory tension. Recital (128) creates a safe harbour for continuous learning changes that were pre-determined and assessed at conformity, even though the specific outcomes cannot be fully predicted. Recital (65) attempts to address this through lifecycle risk management covering 'risks arising from the interaction between the AI system and the environment,' provider documentation obligations, and instruction-for-use requirements covering foreseeable misuse. Recital (155) adds post-market monitoring and serious incident reporting. However, the Act does not specify the threshold at which accumulated continuous learning changes effectively make the system a different system than what was assessed. There is no quantitative or qualitative trigger for re-assessment beyond 'substantial modification.' The scientific panel (Recital 163) and AI Office monitoring (Recital 164) provide systemic risk monitoring but only for GPAI models, not all high-risk AI systems. This gap could theoretically allow a system to drift significantly from its assessed profile while remaining within the 'pre-determined' safe harbour, creating a compliance fiction. The Act relies on post-market monitoring to catch this, but monitoring obligations are on the provider who has an interest in avoiding re-assessment.
