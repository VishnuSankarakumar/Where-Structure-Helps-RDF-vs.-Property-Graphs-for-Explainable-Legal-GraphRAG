The ancillary feature test in Recital (16) requires three conditions: intrinsic linkage to a commercial service, objective technical inability to use without the principal service, and non-circumvention of the Regulation. The first two are potentially objective (technical architecture review), but the third is inherently subjective and intent-based. The Act provides examples (marketplace product preview filters, social media photo filters) but no general methodology for determining circumvention intent. Key gaps include: (1) No definition of 'objective technical reasons' versus deliberate architectural choices that make features inseparable; a company could design tight coupling specifically to invoke the exemption. (2) No burden-of-proof allocation; the Act does not specify whether the provider must demonstrate ancillarity or the authority must demonstrate circumvention. (3) No guidance on how the categorisation capabilities in Recital (30) (political opinions, trade union membership, religious beliefs, race, sex life, sexual orientation) interact with the ancillary exception; it appears the prohibition in Recital (30) would override the ancillary exception for these specific sensitive categories, but this is not explicitly stated. (4) No retrospective analysis requirement; a feature could launch as genuinely ancillary but evolve into a primary function through usage patterns. The Act would benefit from a safe harbour procedure, technical auditing standards for architectural coupling assessment, and clear precedence rules between the prohibition categories and the ancillary exception.
